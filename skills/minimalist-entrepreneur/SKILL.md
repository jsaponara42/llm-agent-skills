# Minimalist Entrepreneur

A comprehensive guide based on *The Minimalist Entrepreneur* by Sahil Lavingia. Covers the full journey from finding your community to building a sustainable business.

Identify where the user is in their journey and apply the relevant framework below. If their need is unclear, ask which stage applies.

---

## Stage 1: Find Your Community

**Core Principle:** Start with community, not with a product idea. The best minimalist businesses are built by people already embedded in a community who notice a problem worth solving.

### Framework: Identify Your Communities

Walk the user through:

1. **What communities are you already a part of?** Professional groups, hobbies, online forums, local organizations, alumni networks, etc.
2. **Where do you spend time online?** Reddit, Discord, Slack, Twitter/X, forums, Substacks, YouTube communities.
3. **What problems do you hear people complain about repeatedly?** The best ideas come from persistent, recurring pain points you understand deeply.
4. **Which communities would you be excited to serve for years?**

### Evaluation Criteria

For each community:
- **Are you a genuine member?** You should understand the language, values, and culture.
- **Is the problem painful enough that people would pay?** The bar: would people exchange money for a solution?
- **Can you reach these people?** Do you know where they gather?
- **Is the community large enough but not too large?** A niche you can dominate.

### Anti-patterns
- Inventing a community from scratch instead of joining an existing one
- Choosing a community purely for market size, not genuine interest
- Jumping straight to "what can I sell" without participating first
- Targeting too broad an audience

### Output
Help the user narrow to 1–3 communities they could realistically serve. For each: the community, the persistent problem, how the user is connected, and where it gathers.

## Stage 2: Validate Your Idea

**Core Principle:** Validation happens through selling, not building. Validate by selling a manual version first.

### The Minimalist Validation Process

**Step 1: Define the Problem (not the solution)**
- Who *specifically* has this problem? (e.g., "freelance graphic designers who struggle with invoicing")
- How are they solving it today? (The current workaround is your real competition)
- How painful is it? (Mild annoyance vs. hair-on-fire)
- Would they pay to make it go away?

**Step 2: Can You Solve It Manually First?**
- "Processize" — create a manual valuable process before writing any code
- Do it yourself first. Write down every step on a piece of paper.
- Example: Gumroad started with Sahil manually collecting PayPal info and paying creators one by one.

**Step 3: Will People Pay?**
- Can you charge for this manual service right now?
- Have you talked to at least 10 potential customers?
- Have at least 3 said they'd pay (or actually paid)?

**Step 4: Four Questions to Ask Before Building**
1. Can I ship it in a weekend?
2. Is it making my customers' life a little better?
3. Is a customer willing to pay for it?
4. Can I get feedback quickly?

### Red Flags (Do Not Build If...)
- Nobody is currently solving this problem
- You can't name 10 specific people who have this problem
- The only validation is "my friends think it's a cool idea"
- You're building for a community you don't belong to

### Green Flags (Worth Pursuing If...)
- People are already paying for inferior solutions
- You've manually solved this for a few people and they loved it
- The community is actively complaining about this problem
- You're scratching your own itch

### Output
Give the user a verdict: **Validated** (proceed to MVP), **Needs more validation** (specific next steps), or **Pivot** (suggest directions).

## Stage 3: Build Your MVP

**Core Principle:** Build as little as possible. Start delivering value as quickly as possible — not something beautiful or complete.

### The Three Stages

1. **Manual** — Solve the problem by hand for each customer. You are the product. Write down every step.
2. **Processized** — Document your process so anyone could do it. This is your "magic piece of paper."
3. **Productized** — Automate the process. Only build what you've already proven works manually.

### The Four Build Questions
Before building anything:
1. Can I ship it in a weekend?
2. Is it making my customers' life a little better?
3. Is a customer willing to pay for it?
4. Can I get feedback quickly?

### What to Build
- **One thing.** Your product does one thing at first.
- **No polish.** It doesn't need to be pretty.
- **Charge money.** There's a huge difference between free and $1.
- **Use existing tools.** Carrd, Gumroad, Stripe, Airtable, Notion, Zapier — whatever gets you to market fastest.

### What NOT to Build
- Features you think you'll need "someday"
- For scale — you don't have scale problems yet
- A mobile app when a website works
- Code when a spreadsheet works

### Essentials Checklist
- [ ] Name your business (two real words > made-up word; pass the "radio test")
- [ ] Buy a domain (~$10/year)
- [ ] Build a simple website (Carrd, Gumroad, or similar)
- [ ] Create social media accounts (personal + business)
- [ ] Set up payments (Stripe or Square)
- [ ] Create an email for customer communication

### Output
Help the user define: the single thing their MVP does, the simplest possible implementation, what they can ship this weekend, their initial price point, and how they'll collect feedback.

## Stage 4: Processize Your Idea

**Core Principle:** Processize before you productize. Every great product started as someone doing the work by hand.

### Step 1: Strip Down the Idea
- What is the **one thing** this product does for a customer?
- What does the customer have before using it, and after?
- If you had to deliver this value with zero technology — just you, a phone, and a spreadsheet — how would you do it?

### Step 2: Who Needs This Today?
- Who is already trying to solve this problem with a workaround?
- Can you name 10 specific people who have this problem right now? If not, go back to Stage 1.

### Step 3: Design the Manual Version
Walk through exactly how you'd deliver the value by hand:
- **What does the customer give you?** (Email, file, description)
- **What do you do with it?** (Every step, in order — be specific)
- **What do you give back?** (The deliverable)
- **How long does it take?** (Per customer)

### Step 4: Write the Magic Piece of Paper
Document your manual process so clearly that someone else could do it. Include:
1. **Trigger** — What kicks off the process?
2. **Steps** — Numbered list of exactly what to do, in order
3. **Tools needed** — What you use at each step
4. **Time per customer** — How long each step takes
5. **Handoff** — How you deliver the result back

### Step 5: Charge for It
The zero price effect means free users give you zero signal. Charge even $1 from day one.

### Step 6: When to Productize
Only automate what you've proven works manually. Signs you're ready:
- You've delivered the manual version to 10+ paying customers
- You can predict exactly what they'll ask for
- Steps on your magic piece of paper haven't changed in weeks
- You're spending more time on delivery than finding customers

### Output
Help the user create: a one-sentence description of what their product does manually, the full magic piece of paper (step by step), a list of 3 specific people to deliver it to this week, their initial price point, and the first step to automate when ready.

## Stage 5: Find Your First 100 Customers

**Core Principle:** Skip the launch. Focus on selling. Sell one by one, learn from each interaction, and build momentum.

### The Concentric Circles of Sales

**Circle 1: Friends and Family**
- Start here. Pitch them on being your first customers, not investors.
- Ask for honest feedback, not social media posts.

**Circle 2: Your Community**
1. Make a list of everyone who has written or shared anything about a similar problem
2. Contact them all personally — walk them through your product
3. Ask for candid feedback (not reviews)

**Circle 3: Strangers (Cold Outreach)**
- Cold emails and messages work. This is how Gumroad grew.
- Example cold email: *"Hi [Name], I saw you're [doing X manually]. I built [product] which automates this. I'd love to show it to you — [link]. Best, [Name]"*
- Don't copy/paste. Each email refines your ability to write better ones.

### Sales is Not a Dirty Word
- You're not convincing anyone. You're helping people.
- Turn every failed conversion into an insight.

### Key Metrics
- Manual sales = 99% of early growth. Word of mouth = 99% of later growth.
- You need far fewer customers than you think. If your product costs $10/month, you need 200 customers for $2,000/month.

### When to "Launch"
Don't launch until you have 100 paying customers. Then launch as a celebration of your community's support.

### Output
Help the user create: a list of 10 friends/family to pitch this week, a list of 10 community members to reach out to, a cold outreach template, their initial pricing strategy, and a weekly sales goal.

## Stage 6: Set Your Pricing

**Core Principle:** Charge something. Always. There is a massive difference between free and $1 (the "zero price effect").

### Two Pricing Models

**1. Cost-Based Pricing**
- Calculate your costs + add a margin (20–50% is typical)
- Best for: physical products, services with clear costs

**2. Value-Based Pricing**
- Price based on value to the customer, not your costs
- Best for: software, digital products, high perceived-value services

### Pricing Principles
1. **Start low, raise over time.** Prices generally go up as products improve.
2. **Pricing is not permanent.** It's just another thing to iterate on.
3. **Tiered pricing is the goal.** Economy, business, first class. Same destination, different experience.
4. **Never give your product away free as a default.**
5. **Free trials are table stakes** — but always with a clear path to paid.

### The Math of Financial Independence
- How much do you need per month?
- At your price point, how many customers is that?
- At one new customer per business day (260/year), when do you hit that number?

### Output
Help the user determine: their pricing model, an initial price point with rationale, potential tier structure for the future, customers needed for financial independence, and when to revisit prices.

## Stage 7: Create Your Marketing Plan

**Core Principle:** Marketing is sales at scale. Start by spending time, not money. Only spend money after you know exactly who you're trying to reach.

### Prerequisites
Before marketing: ~100 customers, product-market fit (repeat customers), experience selling one-on-one.

### The Marketing Funnel
1. **Engage** — They encounter your content
2. **Follow** — They find you interesting enough to follow
3. **Research** — They check out your product
4. **Consider** — They evaluate pricing and features
5. **Buy** — They become a customer

### The Three Levels of Content

**Level 1: Educate**
- Share what you've learned from building your business
- If you have 100 customers, you've learned 100 things worth sharing
- Doesn't need to be polished — just consistent

**Level 2: Inspire**
- Share your journey, struggles, and successes
- Be vulnerable and authentic
- Document progress, not just expertise

**Level 3: Entertain**
- The hardest but most far-reaching
- Think: jokes, patterns, surprises

### Social Media Guide
- Create two accounts: personal (you) and business (the company)
- Don't share status updates — share ideas
- Build in public: share what you're working on
- Pick one platform that works for your business

### Email: Own Your Audience
- Social media = rented land. Email = owned land.
- Start building an email list immediately
- Each email subscriber is worth far more than a social media follower

### Spend Money Last
- Only spend on ads after organic traction and knowing your customer profile
- Don't pay more per customer than you make from them

### Output
Help the user create: their primary content platform and posting schedule, 5 content ideas per level (educate/inspire/entertain), an email list strategy, a "build in public" plan, and when (if ever) to consider paid advertising.

## Stage 8: Grow Sustainably

**Core Principle:** Profitability is a superpower. Spend less than you make.

### The Equation
**Profit = Revenue - Costs**

### Two Types of Costs
- **Variable (COGS):** Scale with each unit sold — payment processing, hosting
- **Fixed:** Don't scale with revenue — domain, people. The #1 fixed cost is always people.

### Cost-Cutting Rules
1. Pay yourself as little as possible, at least to start
2. Hire software, not humans (Pilot, Gusto, Zapier)
3. Don't get an office
4. Outsource everything — freelancers before full-time employees

### Growth Mindset
- You don't need to dominate the market or disrupt anything
- Your company will grow as quickly as your customers determine
- Working more hours doesn't necessarily mean faster growth

### Fundraising (If You Must)
- Bootstrap first — profitability gives you leverage
- Consider Regulation Crowdfunding — turn customers into investors
- New VC alternatives: Earnest Capital, Indie.vc, Tinyseed Fund

### Avoiding Burnout
Two fatal mistakes: running out of money, and running out of energy.
- Treat co-founder relationships like a marriage — have hard conversations early, use vesting
- Don't treat your business as all-or-nothing

### When Profitable
- Infinite runway
- Ship slowly and thoughtfully
- Others may rocket past you on vanity metrics, but you'll still be around in 10 years

### Output
For any business decision, evaluate: impact on profitability, reversibility, whether it's driven by customer needs or ego, cheaper/simpler alternatives, and the "default alive or default dead" test.

## Stage 9: Define Company Values

**Core Principle:** Focus on culture before hiring. Values are not generic two-word commandments — they state the non-obvious, in non-obvious ways.

### Why Values Matter
- Tell employees how to behave every day AND in extreme situations
- Attract the right people and repel the wrong ones — both are valuable
- Let you hold yourself and your team accountable
- Allow the company to scale beyond your personal involvement

### How to Create Your Own Values

Ask:
1. **What do you believe that most people don't?** Values should be non-obvious and sometimes polarizing.
2. **How should people behave when no one is watching?**
3. **What would you fire someone for, even if they're performing well?** That reveals your true values.
4. **What would you celebrate, even if it didn't help the bottom line?**
5. **Write them as stories, not slogans.** "Focus on the user" is a slogan. Nordstrom accepting tire returns at a clothing store is a value told through story.

### Operationalizing Values
- Communicate them publicly — in job posts, on your website, in onboarding
- Use them in feedback: "This aligns with our value of X" or "This doesn't reflect our value of Y"
- Revisit regularly — values evolve as your company grows

### Output
Help the user draft: 3–5 company values with descriptions and example stories, how each shows up in hiring decisions, how each shows up in day-to-day work, and anti-patterns for each value.

## Stage 10: Minimalist Review (Gut-Check Any Decision)

**Core Principle:** Apply minimalist entrepreneur principles to evaluate any business decision.

### The Eight Principles

1. **Community First** — Does this serve your community, or is it ego/vanity?
2. **Start Manual, Then Automate** — Could this be done manually first? Are you over-building?
3. **Build as Little as Possible** — Can you ship this in a weekend? What's the simplest version?
4. **Sell Before You Scale** — Have real people paid real money for this?
5. **Spend Time Before Money** — Can you do this with time instead of money?
6. **Profitability is the Goal** — Does this bring you closer to or further from profitability?
7. **Grow at the Speed of Your Customers** — Are your customers asking for this?
8. **Build the House You Want to Live In** — Does this align with your values?

### Decision Framework

For any decision, evaluate:

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Does this serve my community/customers? | |
| Is this the simplest approach? | |
| Does this improve profitability? | |
| Is this reversible if it doesn't work? | |
| Am I spending time or money? | |
| Have customers asked for this? | |
| Does this align with my values? | |
| Will I still want this in a year? | |

### Common Minimalist Entrepreneur Advice

- "Don't launch. Sell to your first 100 customers first."
- "Hire when it hurts."
- "Profitability gets you off the grid. Then you grow mindfully with unlimited runway."
- "Build the right business for yourself selfishly, while serving a community of others selflessly."

### Output
Give the user: a clear recommendation (do it / don't do it / simplify it), what the minimalist version of their plan looks like, the biggest risk to watch for, and one thing to try this week to validate the decision.
